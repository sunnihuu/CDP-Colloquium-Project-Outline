Situated Technology
  • Computation as Embedded Practice: Technologies like GIS and D3.js are not neutral—they reflect and shape the social, political, and ecological systems in which they operate.
  • Critical Cartography: Maps are arguments, not representations—they reveal power, inequality, and movement.
  • Infrastructural Aesthetics: Digital interfaces are used to expose the hidden infrastructures behind food systems—trade routes, labor flows, environmental costs.
  • Hybrid Methods: Combines GIS metrics, migration data, food accessibility indices, and community interviews to build multi-layered narratives.
  • Qualitative + Quantitative: Data is not just numbers—it includes memory, stories, lived experience, and local knowledge.
  • Web as Spatial Interface: The browser becomes a layered, interactive map for unfolding systemic and personal food journeys.
  • Design as Exposure: Making visible the slow violence, extractive circuits, and uneven access embedded in everyday meals.
  • Urban Justice Orientation: Investigating who gets access to what food, in what neighborhoods, and under what conditions.

Rhetorical Argument

Through the layered mapping of food systems, my project argues that food access and ecological footprints are deeply intertwined with historical power dynamics and infrastructural invisibilities. Design is not neutral: it is a tool to expose, challenge, and reimagine these embedded inequalities. By combining quantitative data with lived narratives, the project advocates for more just, transparent, and participatory urban food futures.

Brief Outline of a Potential Capstone Project
  • Part 1: Mapping NYC Foodprint
    • Collect and integrate multi-source data, including government datasets, environmental indicators, and community inputs.
    • Use GIS and computational design tools to create a multi-layered digital map showing food system spatial distribution, environmental impacts, and socioeconomic disparities.
    • Perform quantitative and qualitative analysis to reveal inequalities in food access, invisible infrastructure, and climate change effects on the food system.
    • Develop interactive or visual artifacts to communicate the complexity of the urban food footprint.
  • Part 2: Hypotheses and Open-Ended Explorations
    • Based on the mapping and analysis, formulate several open hypotheses such as:
      • Digital service platforms could improve food accessibility in underserved communities.
      • Targeted infrastructure upgrades in specific neighborhoods might alleviate food insecurity.
      • Supply chain transparency combined with environmental data could encourage sustainable consumption behaviors.
    • Explore diverse future design possibilities, including service system updates, digital platforms, and physical infrastructure improvements.
    • Maintain an open, exploratory stance without locking into a single solution prematurely.
  • Project Intentions
    • Combine data-driven research with design thinking to produce insightful and inspiring outcomes.
    • Provide multi-dimensional perspectives and future imaginaries for ongoing urban food system research and interventions.
    • Emphasize the complexity of spatial justice and sustainability while encouraging interdisciplinary collaboration and community engagement.
