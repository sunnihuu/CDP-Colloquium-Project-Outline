Student Name
Mapping NYC Foodprint: Making Urban Food Carbon Footprints Visible and Actionable
Research Question(s)
  • How can we break the carbon-intensive cycles of urban food systems through data, systems thinking, and computational design?
  • What spatial and systemic inequalities exist in NYC’s food system, and how can we make them visible to promote equitable and sustainable practices?
  • How can civic interfaces translate data awareness into behavioral and policy shifts?

Historical Lineage
This project continues a lineage of critical cartography, civic data visualization, and environmental systems analysis. It builds on:
  • Laura Kurgan’s spatial justice and food mapping
  • The tradition of urban metabolism modeling
  • Shannon Mattern’s work on infrastructural legibility
  • Safiya Noble’s interrogation of algorithmic inequality
  • Visual storytelling practices from The Pudding, NYT, and Columbia’s Center for Spatial Research
It emerges from debates around food justice, climate equity, and environmental urbanism, responding to the increasing urgency of localizing sustainability efforts within the infrastructures of everyday life.
My Community of Practice
Precedents: Observatory of Economic Complexity (OEC), The Haven Project (CSR), NYT’s interactive visualizations, Klimato, Too Good To Go
Practitioners & Projects: Data for Black Lives, The Center for Spatial Research, Project Drawdown, MIT’s Senseable City Lab, The Pudding
Relation: My project draws from these models but aims to make foodprint mapping more personal, place-based, and intervention-oriented—bridging awareness and action for individuals and communities.
Audience: While some practices are aimed at policy and academia, my audience also includes NYC residents, small business owners, and grassroots organizers.
Situated Technology
This project aligns with CDP’s focus on computation as a mode of critical spatial practice. By treating interface as exposure, it frames food as a medium of slow violence and hidden inequity. The goal is not merely to inform but to activate: to design for collective responsibility and to model interfaces that make systemic patterns actionable.
Methods
Software Tools: Python (Pandas, GeoPandas), Rhino + Grasshopper, QGIS, Mapbox, D3.js, Figma
Approaches:
  • Quantitative: carbon modeling, emissions aggregation by geography

Components:
  • Computational: mapping & modeling workflows
  • Representational: interactive & scroll-based visuals

Rhetorical Argument
Design can make visible the invisible burdens embedded in daily urban food consumption—and expose the systemic cycles that reproduce them. By visualizing carbon inequities and coupling them with interfaces for action, we can shift from passive awareness to collective, place-based responsibility. Civic data is not neutral—it can empower, advocate, and repair.
Brief Outline of a Potential Capstone Project
Title: Footprint Wallet: Mapping and Reducing NYC’s Urban Foodprint
Structure:
  • Part 1: Mapping the Footprint
    • Data visualization platform showing carbon intensity by cuisine, neighborhood, and socioeconomic factors
    • Use-case filters (residents, policy makers, restaurant owners)
    • Scroll-based map and storytelling tools
  • Part 2: Designing for Intervention
    • Prototyped tools for:
      • Restaurant sustainability labeling
      • Low-cost climate-friendly food discovery
      • Community-based footprint reduction (fridges, leftovers, swaps)
    • Behavior-change UX linked to local infrastructure (markets, apps)
The Challenge
Data Limitations: Gaps in granular emissions data and food access infrastructure datasets; merging multiple sources into coherent systems
Technical Skill: Continued development in D3.js, geospatial modeling, and full-stack web interaction for prototyping
Scientific Knowledge: Translating complex carbon accounting frameworks into simplified public metrics
Design Communication: Balancing clarity and nuance; designing for diverse literacy and device contexts
Next Steps: Continue building modular tools, test mini-prototypes with local partners, develop interface dramaturgy practices
