Historical Lineage

This project emerges from the intersection of urban data practices, food justice movements, and design as inquiry. It builds on traditions of using spatial tools, particularly GIS and footprint modeling, to make hidden infrastructures visible and to expose the social and environmental costs embedded in everyday systems.

The focus lies on the urban food system and its associated foodprint: the carbon, water, and land impacts of food production, distribution, access, and waste. These impacts are often invisible, yet spatially distributed and systemically produced. Mapping them becomes a way to uncover how environmental burdens and urban inequalities are intertwined.

This project will follow a lineage of critical cartography and urban political ecology, where maps are not neutral representations but tools to question, interpret, and intervene. The project draws from frameworks such as critical infrastructure studies, food sovereignty, and urban metabolism, while aligning with contemporary design approaches including ecological data visualization and community-centered urban prototyping. It continues a trajectory of data-driven urbanism, where making the invisible visible becomes a first step toward more just and embodied futures.

My Community of Practice

Below are key precedent projects, tools, and organizations that have shaped the field of food systems mapping, foodprints, and spatial food infrastructure. They represent diverse approaches from policy to design, from GIS to environmental visualization.

  • Foodprint NYC — by Nicola Twilley & Sarah Rich
    Early attempt to map the "invisible landscape" of food infrastructure in New York City, combining speculative design with systems thinking.
  • Edible Infrastructures — AA EmTech Thesis
    Architectural proposal turning infrastructure into productive edible systems, exploring hydroponics, vertical farming, and metabolic design.
  • Nourishing Territories — Montréal Food System Mapping
    GIS-based spatial analysis of food access, Indigenous food sovereignty, and community-based urban planning in Canada.
  • Foodprint.org
    Non-profit organization focusing on environmental and ethical impacts of food. Promotes foodprint as a concept, provides educational content.
  • NYC Food Policy Center
    Researches urban food policy, sustainability, justice, and resilience. Notable projects include school meal programs, COVID response, and food access maps.
  • Our World in Data
    Offers global food-related emissions, land use, and resource consumption data. Useful as parameters for NYC regional modeling.
  • FAO’s Foodprint Calculator
    Provides baseline life-cycle data on food emissions and land impacts. Used for national and regional footprint calculations.
  • Barilla Center for Food & Nutrition – Food Sustainability Index
    Evaluates cities/countries by food waste, footprint, and nutritional access metrics. Useful for comparative urban analysis.
  • Cool Food Pledge — by World Resources Institute (WRI)
    Framework and LCA database to reduce food-related emissions. Offers detailed coefficients and footprint visual standards.
  • C40 Cities – Food and Climate Report
    Urban climate governance network. The food report explores how cities like NYC can reduce food-based GHG emissions through policy and procurement.
  • Google Environmental Insights Explorer
    Though not food-specific, provides city-level carbon footprint mapping. Useful for visual inspiration and modeling urban emissions context.

Rhetorical Argument

This project argues that the true infrastructures of urban food systems are largely invisible, yet they profoundly shape environmental outcomes, social inequalities, and cultural identities. By making these hidden dynamics visible through mapping and design, we can challenge dominant narratives that treat food as a neutral commodity and instead reveal the complex networks of labor, migration, and ecological impact behind what we eat.

The project contends that understanding the “foodprint” is not only a matter of calculating carbon or water use, but also of recognizing how spatial justice, cultural memory, and community agency are embedded in food flows. Design here becomes a form of activism: a call to reimagine food infrastructures as equitable, legible, and edible, inviting new possibilities for urban sustainability and justice.

Brief Outline of a Potential Capstone Project
  • Project Focus:
    • Deepen investigation of NYC’s urban food system and its environmental foodprint.
    • Integrate geospatial data with community narratives to reveal hidden food flows and environmental burdens.
  • Research Methods:
    • Continue GIS spatial analysis incorporating carbon, water, and land footprint data.
    • Conduct qualitative interviews and oral histories to capture immigrant food cultures.
    • Develop multi-layered, interactive visualizations combining data and storytelling.
  • Key Questions:
    • How do food access and environmental impacts vary across different communities?
    • How do food migration paths reflect cultural, economic, and ecological intersections?
    • How can design increase transparency and equity within food systems?
  • Project Outputs:
    • Web-based interactive mapping platform showcasing food flows, footprints, and community stories.
    • Dynamic data visualizations revealing key nodes and hidden relations in the food system.
    • Design prototypes such as a low-carbon food navigation app or interactive models of community food infrastructures.
  • Project Approach:
    • Maintain an open exploratory stance without fixed solutions at this stage.
    • Use design as a mode of inquiry and dialogue to provoke reflection on complex systems.
  • Engagement and Impact:
    • Provide accessible tools and visuals for community members, designers, and policymakers.
    • Facilitate public conversations about food justice and environmental sustainability.
    • Lay groundwork for future design interventions and policy recommendations.
The Challenge
ul>
  • Data Accessibility: Food migration and distribution datasets are fragmented and often inconsistent across different cities or agencies.
    Plan: Compile data from public sources (NYC Open Data, USDA, UN FAO) and consider qualitative supplements via interviews or community mapping.
  • Technical Learning Curve: Need to improve advanced data visualization (D3.js), GIS analytics, and API integrations.
    Plan: Complete targeted tutorials, use small weekly prototypes, and consult peers with stronger dev backgrounds.
  • Visual Communication: Crafting a clear and engaging aesthetic that supports argumentation while handling complex multi-layer data.
    Plan: Reference best practices from Datasketch, Polygraph, and The Pudding.
  • Community Relationships: Grounding the work in lived experience requires trust-building with cultural communities and food practitioners.
    Plan: Reach out to local mutual aid groups, urban gardens, or food policy councils in NYC neighborhoods.
  • Scope & Focus: Balancing scale (global migration vs. hyperlocal mapping) without losing coherence.
    Plan: Prioritize one core migration case (e.g. the journey of rice or chili pepper) to anchor the interface and argument.
