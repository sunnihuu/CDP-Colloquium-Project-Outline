Research Question(s)
  • How can we use data, design, and computation to make the carbon footprint of NYC’s food system visible to both policy makers and the public?
  • What spatial, economic, and infrastructural patterns make food consumption more carbon-intensive in some communities than others?
  • How can interface design translate systems awareness into equitable, actionable change?
  • How might interactive storytelling and civic visualization tools influence behaviors, support advocacy, and inform low-carbon food choices?
  • What would it mean to prototype urban food technologies not just for efficiency or convenience, but for sustainability and justice?

Situated Technology
This project responds to the CDP theme by positioning technology as a situated, civic instrument—capable of exposing unseen violence (e.g., environmental burden, food deserts), enabling informed agency, and building accountability.
Inspired by readings and discussions in the colloquium, this work treats interface design as a form of spatial exposure—one that doesn’t neutralize complexity, but makes it legible and actionable across social divides.
Key influences:
“Design as Exposure” → visualize hidden systems, slow violence, extractive circuits
Civic UX → not designing for control, but for relational, public forms of understanding

Rhetorical Argument
"Every meal is a climate act."

My project argues that urban food systems are both the site of harm and opportunity. By tracing and visualizing their environmental impact at a granular level, we can:
  • Reveal hidden burdens of food inequity
  • Highlight actionable levers across the chain (from menus to fridges)
  • Shift food from being a private, invisible act to a civic, collective one
Design here is not about building a “solution” to food emissions—it’s about making visible the carbon patterns we inherit and sustain, so we can collectively imagine alternatives.
Capstone Project Proposal (Preview)
Tentative Title: Footprint Wallet: A Civic Tool to Map, Reduce, and Share Low-Carbon Food Practices
Components:
  • 1. Emission Mapping Platform
    Public-facing scroll-based website with NYC neighborhood foodprint explorer
    Emission overlays by cuisine, geography, income
  • 2. Restaurant Dashboard Prototype
    Menu calculator for carbon scoring
    “Low-carbon badge” system
    Map of sustainable eateries
  • 3. Community-Facing App Concepts
    “Foodprint Wallet” for residents to track impact and discover low-cost, low-impact food
    Leftover tracking + community fridge finder
    Reward/incentive integration for behavior change
Outcome: An ecosystem of digital tools that translate foodprint awareness into individual and collective action.
